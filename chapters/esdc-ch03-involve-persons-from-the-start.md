# Play 3: Involve persons with disabilities from the start

## Core Idea
You cannot design accessible services *for* people without designing *with* them. The insights that matter most won't come from reading accessibility standards — they come from real persons with disabilities participating in research, co-design, and feedback from day one.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Three Engagement Pillars** — build all three, not just one:
  1. **Recruitment of persons with disabilities** — partner with NGOs/advocacy groups for UX testing; use *multiple* recruitment approaches because any single method excludes some profiles.
  2. **Engagement & consultations** — build long-term relationships with advocacy groups; leverage their expertise, tools, networks for *continuous* improvement.
  3. **Feedback mechanisms** — provide feedback options across every channel (verbal, email, phone, surveys, social, shadowing, focus groups); ensure front-line staff know how to route informal feedback to someone who can act.

- **Gather Rich Client Data** — combine multiple insight sources to support decisions:
  - Choose a random sample that genuinely represents persons with disabilities.
  - Review whether current methods actually reach clients.
  - Ensure the co-design process is itself accessible.
  - Use **journey mapping** to surface barriers across channels and validate solutions.
  - Run an **early accessibility assessment** before building.

## Key Concepts
- **Client insights** — in-depth knowledge of clients (demographic, behavioural, needs-based, attitudes, feelings) from environmental scans and research.
- **Co-design** — designing alongside the people who will use the service; only valid if the process is accessible.
- **Consultation fatigue** — over-asking the same client groups; avoid it by coordinating engagement across departments with similar needs.

## Mental Models
- **"Outliers may be the signal, not the noise."** Don't dismiss disability feedback as white noise or data errors — each disability is different.
- **Standards tell you the rules; people tell you the experience.** Documentation can't teach you how to create a great CX for someone with cognitive or physical challenges.
- **A small % of a large program is still a lot of people** — and may be those most in need (see Ch 4).

## Anti-patterns
- **Single recruitment channel** — guarantees you miss some disability profiles.
- **Treating disability feedback as error/noise** to be cleaned out of the data.
- **Consultation fatigue** from uncoordinated, repetitive outreach.

## Key Takeaways
1. Recruit through partners; use multiple methods to ensure diversity.
2. Build long-term relationships with advocacy groups, not one-off consults.
3. Offer feedback options on every channel — and make sure staff can escalate it.
4. Use journey mapping + early assessment to find barriers before launch.
5. Make the co-design process accessible, or it isn't co-design.
