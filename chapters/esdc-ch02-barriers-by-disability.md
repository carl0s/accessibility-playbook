# Play 2: Understand how barriers affect persons with disabilities

## Core Idea
The same service feels different depending on the disability AND the channel (in-person / phone / online). To understand barriers, walk each client group through all three channels — this play does exactly that for six personas ("Billy's friends").

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Channel × Disability Matrix**: for every client group, map the *possible in-person*, *phone*, and *online* experience. This is the playbook's core diagnostic lens — no single channel is accessible to everyone, so always offer alternatives.
- **The Six Personas (Billy's friends)** — use as ready-made empathy lenses in design:
  - **Mark, 25 — physical disability**: pain, fatigue, dexterity. Counter height, signing pens on strings, holding a phone are hard.
  - **Nora, 49 — mental-health condition**: focus/memory; IVR trees overwhelm; forms feel "too long and complex."
  - **Luca, 31 — cognitive disability**: slower processing, needs text explained, prefers in-person; legal-capacity challenges create exclusion.
  - **Jeremy, 44 — communication disability**: slurred/paused speech mistaken for intoxication or prank calls; uses AAC.
  - **Léo, 67 — severe hearing loss**: sign-language grammar ≠ English/French; needs TTY/VRS, captions, "speak clearly, not loudly."
  - **Sophie, 36 — visual impairment**: not all blind; needs alt tags, tactile/Braille signage, high-contrast, beacon wayfinding.

## Key Concepts
- **AAC (Augmentative and Alternative Communication)** — gestures, pictures, letter boards, communication devices.
- **TTY / TDD** — Text Telephone for Deaf/hard-of-hearing users; perceived as rarely answered with equal frequency.
- **VRS / VRI** — Video Relay Service / Video Remote Interpreting for sign-language users.
- **IVR (Interactive Voice Response)** — option-tree phone menus; a major barrier for cognitive/mental-health/hearing groups.
- **Counter loop** — induction loop that feeds audio directly to hearing aids, cutting background noise.

## Mental Models
- **"Don't shout — speak clearly and naturally at a moderate pace"** (Léo): louder ≠ clearer.
- **Slurred speech ≠ intoxication** (Jeremy): never infer disability behaviour as misconduct.
- **A condition is not a constant.** Symptoms fluctuate; what a client could do last time may differ this time.

## Anti-patterns
- **Long IVR option trees** with no "press 0 for a live agent" — traps cognitive, mental-health, and Deaf callers.
- **Time-bound online forms** — block anyone needing breaks; forms should "save and continue later."
- **Improvements that create new barriers** — bright flashes signal hearing-impaired users but can trigger seizures; check frequency.
- **Assuming a companion will do tasks for the client** — clients have the right (and privacy) to self-serve.

## Key Takeaways
1. Always evaluate a service across all three channels for each disability group.
2. Offer "press 0" to reach a live agent on every phone tree.
3. Never make online forms time-bound; allow save-and-resume.
4. Authorize agents to fill out forms and provide signature guides on the client's behalf.
5. Use the six personas as quick empathy checks during design reviews.
6. Stats anchor urgency: 65% with physical disability live with daily pain; 71% have more than one disability type.
