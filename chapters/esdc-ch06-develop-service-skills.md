# Play 6: Develop the skills to provide accessible service

## Core Idea
Accessible service is a learnable skill set, not just infrastructure. The default posture is respect and patience: ask before helping, speak directly to the client, use person-first language, and learn the etiquette for support persons, relay calls, and service animals.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Approach clients the right way — Attitude rules**:
  - Ask "**How may I help you?**" — don't assume help is needed or what kind.
  - Don't assume the type of disability or the accommodation needed.
  - Speak **directly to the client**, never to their companion/support person.
  - Listen actively; confirm by summarizing/repeating; be patient (some take longer).
  - Some disabilities are invisible; clients need not disclose.

- **Person-First Language swaps** (say this, not that):
  - "the blind / the visually impaired" → **person who is blind / person with a visual impairment**
  - "the hard of hearing / deaf-mute / hearing impaired" → **person who is hard of hearing / person who is deaf**
  - "the handicapped" → **person with a disability**
  - "handicapped parking/bathrooms" → **accessible parking / accessible bathrooms**

- **TTY / VRS interaction protocol**: speak directly to the client (not the relay operator/interpreter); talk a bit slower; spell names; for TTY say "**Go Ahead**" when finished and wait for the operator's "Go Ahead" before speaking.

- **Support-person etiquette**: permit them on premises; allow them at visits/calls if the client requests; get the client's permission before discussing confidential info in front of them; look at and speak to the client even when they communicate through the support person.

- **Service-animal etiquette**: it's not a pet — treat it as an extension of the owner's arm. Don't pet, talk to, feed, or startle it. Don't ask the owner to leave it elsewhere. The owner controls it; you're not responsible for its care (may offer water if asked). Not all wear harnesses — it's okay to ask if it's a service animal.

## Key Concepts
- **Service animal types** — guide dog (vision), hearing/signal (alerts to sound), mobility assistance, seizure response, therapeutic assistance.
- **Mandatory training topics** — the Accessible Canada Act; common disability types; interacting with persons with disabilities / assistive devices / support persons; using equipment (screen readers, VRI, TTY); reading this playbook.
- **Role-specific training** — beyond general awareness/sensitivity (e.g. ESDC's "The Richness of our Differences").

## Mental Models
- **"The client knows if they need help and how."** Offer, don't impose.
- **Speak to the person, not the proxy** — companion, interpreter, or relay operator.
- **Behaviour ≠ misconduct** — slurred speech, odd body language, or escalation may be disability-related; handle with sensitivity.

## Anti-patterns
- **Touching or addressing a service animal** — distracts it from its job.
- **Talking to the support person/interpreter instead of the client.**
- **Outdated terminology** ("the handicapped", "deaf-mute") — use person-first language.
- **Assuming the accommodation** before asking.

## Key Takeaways
1. Ask "How may I help you?" — never assume.
2. Always use person-first language.
3. Speak directly to the client through any intermediary.
4. Learn TTY/VRS protocol ("Go Ahead", spell names, slow down).
5. Don't interact with service animals; the owner is in control.
6. General awareness training for all; role-specific training for front-line staff.
