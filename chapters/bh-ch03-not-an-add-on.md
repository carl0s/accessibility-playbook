# [Giving a Damn] Ch 3: Accessibility is not an add-on service

## Core Idea
Accessibility is a specialization built on interpreting regulations — like privacy or security consulting. If a vendor offers it as "one tiny corner" of an unrelated service menu, they're probably not doing it well. This chapter is a buyer's guide for evaluating accessibility consultants (and a warning against treating it as a bolt-on internally).

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Consultant Red Flags** — how new-to-market accessibility vendors give themselves away:
  1. **"Magic wand" claim** — assuming anyone can be turned into an accessibility tester instantly. Reality: certification takes dedication; learning how disabled people use tech takes longer.
  2. **They rarely employ people with disabilities** — or worse, outsource disabled-tester work to charities for "pennies on the dollar." Balance matters; ask the question as a buyer.
  3. **"We can do every type of accessibility testing on the planet."** Claiming to be best at everything signals they don't understand what they're good at (e.g. can't say what PDF/UA stands for).

- **The Specialization Analogy** — would you buy security consulting from a company that also lays bricks? Accessibility consulting deserves the same scrutiny. "One of these things is not like the other."

## Key Concepts
- **Add-on service trap** — accessibility offered as a minor item on an unrelated menu (a contact center "also offering accessibility").
- **PDF/UA** — the accessibility standard for PDFs; a litmus-test acronym for whether a vendor actually knows the field.
- **VPAT (Voluntary Product Accessibility Template)** — a core accessibility-program artifact linking to most org departments (referenced in the author bio).
- **Systemic ableism** — drives disability unemployment as high as 90% in some regions, which is why disabled coworkers "frequently don't exist" outside North America/Europe.

## Mental Models
- **Accessibility is a specialization, not a side dish** — treat it like privacy/security consulting.
- **"Best at everything" = good at nothing.** Depth signals competence; breadth claims signal the opposite.
- **Ask the procurement questions** — it's on the *buyer* to verify the vendor doesn't exploit disabled testers.

## Anti-patterns
- **Buying accessibility from a generalist** bundling it with unrelated services.
- **Vendors who can't define basic terms** (PDF/UA) yet claim full coverage.
- **Exploiting charities/disabled testers** at below-market rates.
- **Treating accessibility testing as optional** rather than standard like usability/performance testing.

## Key Takeaways
1. Vet accessibility consultants like security/privacy specialists — depth over breadth.
2. Red flags: magic-wand claims, no disabled employees, "we do everything."
3. Ask procurement questions before contracting; verify fair treatment of disabled testers.
4. Push your own org to make accessibility a standard practice, not an add-on.
5. Demand from vendors before the engagement starts (asking later is too late).
