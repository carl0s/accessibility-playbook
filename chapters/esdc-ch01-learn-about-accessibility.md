# Play 1: Learn about accessibility, it's more than you think

## Core Idea
Accessibility is the "ability to access" without encountering barriers — and a barrier is any obstacle that prevents a person with a disability from doing what others take for granted. Every public servant has a role in removing them; equitable access should be part of everyday work, not an afterthought.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The 5 Service Barrier Areas**: every barrier to client service falls into one of five areas — use this to audit any program or channel.
  1. **Physical Space** — location, signage out of line-of-sight, background noise, stairs/doorways, furniture as tripping hazards, plexiglas blocking the hard-of-hearing.
  2. **Policies & Processes** — rules that block access (e.g. agent *not* authorized to fill out a form for a client).
  3. **Language & Information** — overly complex text, formats that aren't accessible, forcing screen readers through long privacy statements.
  4. **Awareness & Attitude** — not promoting features, ineffective/untimely communication, untrained staff, assumptions/unfair judgments.
  5. **Technology** — systems that don't support screen readers, accessible keyboards, audio players, or compatibility with clients' home assistive devices. (WCAG is the *starting point* — strive to do better.)
  - When to use: as a checklist whenever evaluating where a service could exclude someone.

- **The 6 Most Common Disability Types**: Physical, Cognitive, Hearing, Mental Health, Communication, Vision. Each can be **permanent, temporary, or episodic**, and **visible or hidden**.

## Key Concepts
- **Barrier** — an obstacle preventing a person with a disability from accessing something others access easily.
- **Episodic disability** — symptoms come and go over time (not constant).
- **Hidden / invisible disability** — no outward sign (e.g. most mental-health conditions, dyslexia, ADHD).
- **Functional limitation** — the practical effect of a condition on a daily task (the framing matters more than the diagnosis).
- **Equitable access** — same service/information where possible; equivalent access where not.

## Mental Models
- **"Everyone benefits from accessibility."** Treat accessibility as good design for all, not a niche accommodation.
- **Think in barriers, not diagnoses.** Design around the obstacle a person hits, not the medical label.
- **WCAG is a floor, not a ceiling.** Meeting the standard is the minimum; inclusion means going beyond it.

## Anti-patterns
- **Treating accessible formats as an afterthought** — they must be designed in, with accessible ways to *obtain* the formats too.
- **Forcing screen-reader users through long privacy statements** that sighted clients simply scroll past — a daily indignity.
- **Assuming a disability is visible** — many are hidden; clients are not required to disclose.

## Key Takeaways
1. Accessibility = barrier-free access + dignity + meaningful options + consistent info across all channels.
2. Audit every service against the 5 barrier areas (Physical, Policies, Language, Attitude, Technology).
3. Disabilities can be permanent/temporary/episodic and visible/hidden — design for all states.
4. WCAG is the entry point, not the goal.
5. It's about respecting human rights — all Canadians are entitled to government services.
