# [Giving a Damn] Ch 2: Your first attempt at making anything accessible will be awful

## Core Idea
Your first accessibility effort won't be outstanding — and that's fine. "Even awful is better than 98% of what other people are doing." The trap isn't being bad at first; it's using that as an excuse to never start. Commitment, not perfection, is the key.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The 5 Truths of the Accessibility Learning Curve**:
  1. **No one starts a new skill at expert level.** Accessibility is like a foreign language or violin — WCAG is dense, multi-solution, and framework/AT-specific (HTML ≠ React ≠ SWIFT; NVDA ≠ JAWS).
  2. **Perfectionism is a bad approach.** Its side effect is complacency ("if I can't be perfect, why bother?"). WCAG is non-prescriptive, so perfect is impossible — decide when something has had *enough* attention and move on.
  3. **Even people with disabilities can't represent everyone.** You see through your own disability lens (three people with identical hearing loss may use sign / hearing aids / captions). Accessibility is **not a zero-sum game** — don't help one group by harming another (e.g. keyboard-only sighted users).
  4. **Success requires opening yourself to criticism.** Don't deflect — desensitize by getting *lots* of feedback and acting on it.
  5. **Keep the needle moving forward.** Accessibility is process improvement, not a project — it never ends; even "right" can be better.

- **The Accessibility Flywheel**: Accessibility compliance → more disabled customers → more accessibility focus → more accessibility features → better operational practices → (back to) compliance. A self-reinforcing loop visited throughout design and development.

## Key Concepts
- **Continuous process improvement loop** — the best accessibility is revisited throughout the entire design/dev lifecycle, surfacing "low-hanging fruit."
- **Bias (neutral sense)** — limited data favoring your own conclusions, not intentional discrimination.
- **"A rising accessibility tide raises all disabled users' boats."**

## Mental Models
- **"Even awful is better than 98% of what others do"** — 98% of websites are completely inaccessible; you can't do worse.
- **Perfectionism → complacency** — waiting for perfect prolongs discrimination.
- **Accessibility is not zero-sum** — never trade one disability group against another.
- **Just start. Commitment is the key.**

## Anti-patterns
- **Using "my first try will be bad" as an excuse** not to begin.
- **Chasing perfection** instead of shipping good-enough-and-improving.
- **Assuming your own disability lens represents all users** with that disability.
- **Deflecting criticism** instead of metabolizing it.
- **Treating accessibility as a one-time project** with an end date.

## Key Takeaways
1. Start now — awful-but-shipping beats the inaccessible 98%.
2. Drop perfectionism; decide "good enough for now," then iterate.
3. Get heavy feedback and desensitize to criticism; act on it.
4. Never make accessibility zero-sum across disability groups.
5. Run a continuous improvement loop (the flywheel), not a project.
