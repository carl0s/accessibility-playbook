# Play 4: Design experiences to be more inclusive

## Core Idea
Universal Design intentionally builds programs and services without creating barriers in the first place. It's not a special accommodation — it's good design that serves everyone. Most policy objectives start accessible and *become* inaccessible through downstream design decisions; the job is to keep checking every decision.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The 7 Principles of Universal Design** (Universal Design Network of Canada):
  1. **Equitable use** — same service/info where possible, equivalent access where not (e.g. no time limits + save-for-later on forms).
  2. **Flexibility in use** — multiple ways to do the same thing (feedback by form, in writing, phone/relay).
  3. **Simple & intuitive** — reduce complexity; accommodate varied literacy/language; signage in alternative formats.
  4. **Perceptible information** — communicate effectively regardless of ability (captions + audio with adjustable size/colour).
  5. **Tolerance for error** — minimize hazards/unintended actions (no obstructing furniture/tripping hazards).
  6. **Low physical effort** — usable efficiently without undue effort (daily accessibility checks; working door openers).
  7. **Size & space for approach and use** — room for standing/seating with assistive devices (multi-level counters).

- **The 3-Lens Channel Checklist** — for in-person, phone, AND online, design across three lenses:
  - **Service Design** (the environment/process), **Assistive Technology** (the tools), **Training & Culture** (the people). See cheatsheet for the concrete items.

- **Intersectionality lens** — a person is more than their disability; identify intersecting characteristics your org may have created barriers for: Indigenous Peoples, poverty, youth, seniors, LGBTQ2+, victims of violence, rural/remote, incarcerated, visible minorities, newcomers, women.

## Key Concepts
- **Curb-cut effect** — an accessibility feature that ends up helping far more people than intended (ramps help strollers, luggage, walking aids).
- **"Leave no one behind"** — a single barrier can block access even if a service is "technically accessible" by standards.
- **It's about the person** — ~60% have conditions that change over time; clients decide how they access services, *every time*.

## Mental Models
- **"Technically accessible" ≠ accessible.** Always ask if the client can *actually* access it.
- **Accessibility decays by decision.** Each design/policy choice can quietly remove access — has every alternative really been considered?
- **No single channel works for everyone** — alternatives are the mechanism of inclusion (1/3 of persons with disabilities struggle with at least one channel).
- **Don't assume a caregiver will act for the client** — that violates their privacy and autonomy.

## Anti-patterns
- **Designing only for "mainstream" clients** — forgets Deaf/blind communities and how they'd even learn the program exists.
- **Plexiglas / stanchions / "stand here" markers** added for safety that create new barriers (lip-reading, cane detection, guide dogs).
- **Forcing screen-reader users to listen to long privacy text** others scroll past.

## Key Takeaways
1. Apply the 7 Universal Design principles as a proactive design rubric.
2. For each channel, cover Service Design + Assistive Tech + Training & Culture.
3. Design for intersectionality — clients have multiple, overlapping characteristics.
4. Always offer more than one channel; one channel is never universally accessible.
5. Re-examine every decision for accessibility decay before launch.
6. During a pandemic/crisis: provide multiple channels, appointments, priority hours, VRI, counterloops; communicate environment changes.
