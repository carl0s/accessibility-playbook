# Play 5: Make communications accessible for everyone

## Core Idea
Plain language and accessible formats make critical information usable by everyone — without dumbing it down. Plain language is the most-overlooked part of accessibility: technical web standards advance while the words themselves stay too complex.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Plain Language — three moves**:
  1. **Choose the right words & sentences** — simple common words; verbs not nominalizations; no jargon/idioms; short, single-idea sentences and paragraphs.
  2. **Inform the right way** — minimize info processed at once; use headings/lists/tables; direct statements; active voice + positive form; explain legislation references.
  3. **Check the reading level** — target **grade 6–8**; above grade 8 becomes hard to understand or complete. Use readability tools.

- **Printed Information — five specs**:
  - **Layout**: logical order, columns, 1.5 line spacing, don't overload.
  - **Colour & contrast**: light-on-dark or reverse; max 3 colours/page; avoid bright colours in text.
  - **Font style/size**: sans-serif (Arial, Helvetica, Verdana); titles 48pt; body 14–16pt.
  - **Format**: matte/uncoated paper to cut glare.

- **Signage — four types**: Wayfinding (space out signs, common terms, keep current), Wall-mounted (numbers/letters, 1.2–1.5 m height, consistent), Technology-based (audio wayfinding, multilingual, text-to-speech), Format (matte finish, tactile + Braille on permanent signs, upper+lowercase, large bold square lettering).

- **Online Information — five rules**: Navigation (adaptive structure, headings for reading order), Images (alt text, no text over busy backgrounds), Audio/Video (transcripts + captions, 20pt subtitles, white-on-black), Content (apply WCAG 2.1, multi-browser/device/AT support), plus avoid excess CAPS/italics/underline.

## Key Concepts
- **Plain language** — clear, simple, concise; NOT over-simplified or stripped of critical info.
- **Alt text** — text describing an image/photo/graph/table for screen readers.
- **Readability tools** — measure text complexity against a grade level.
- **Reading order** — the sequence a screen reader follows; controlled by heading structure.

## Mental Models
- **Plain language is part of WCAG compliance, not separate from it.** A "technically accessible" page in dense prose still fails.
- **Essential info first** — small-screen and assistive-tech users see the top first.
- **One idea per paragraph.**

## Anti-patterns
- **Walls of text** — break into simple sentences and bullets.
- **Figures of speech & idioms** — write plainly instead.
- **Spreading content all over a page** — follow a linear, logical layout.
- **Information only in an image/video** — describe it and provide transcripts.
- **Forcing mouse/screen use** — build for keyboard-only; make large clickable targets, never demand precision.
- **Too many capitals, italics, or underlines** — hurts readability and can change tone/meaning.

## Key Takeaways
1. Write at grade 6–8; always check reading level with a tool.
2. Sans-serif, 14–16pt body / 48pt titles, matte paper, max 3 colours.
3. Alt text on every meaningful image; transcripts + captions on all media.
4. Headings drive screen-reader reading order — use them.
5. Build keyboard-first with large clickable targets.
